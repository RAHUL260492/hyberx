Weekly Recap · Web Design, Dev & SEO

An unconfirmed shakeup, an llms.txt myth busted, and 92% of AI traffic from one source.

13 July 202610 min read

No confirmed Google update this week — and yet rankings jumped anyway. Between 6 and 12 July the SEO world watched an unconfirmed volatility spike, got hard numbers on where AI referral traffic actually comes from, and heard Google flatly dismiss one of 2026's favourite optimisation hacks. Plus a WordPress maintenance release, a Search Console expansion, and a reviews bug that scared local businesses for nothing. If your website is how customers find and choose you, here's the week in full — and the playbook for each item.

1. The "7-Eleven update": big volatility, no confirmation

What happened

Around Saturday 11 July, rank-tracking tools lit up and site owners reported significant ranking swings — enough that the SEO community nicknamed it the "7-Eleven update." Google confirmed nothing. That makes it the second unconfirmed tremor since the June spam update finished on 26 June, and part of a clear 2026 pattern: the algorithm now moves constantly between named updates, not just during them.

The triage sequence when rankings move and Google says nothing

  • Day 1–2: diagnose, don't act. Separate rank changes from click changes in Search Console. A position drop and a click drop have different causes and different fixes.
  • Check the query class. Did you lose brand queries (rare, alarming), commercial queries ("web design company London" — competitive churn), or informational queries (often AI Overview expansion, not a penalty)?
  • Compare competitors. If everyone in your niche moved, it's the algorithm breathing. If only you moved, look at what changed on your site in the last 30 days.
  • Rule out technical causes. Deindexed pages, broken redirects, a slow deploy, an expired plugin — boring causes explain more "algorithm hits" than algorithms do.
  • Day 7+: act on evidence. Only rewrite or restructure once a full week of data shows a sustained pattern.
What we'd do about it

With unconfirmed volatility, the worst move is a panicked rewrite. Sites with strong fundamentals — fast pages, real expertise, clean structure — usually round-trip back within days. If your site gets hit by every tremor, that's the tell that the fundamentals need work, and that's a website design and development problem before it's a content problem.

2. 92% of AI referral traffic comes from one place

A study of 6.77 million website visits put numbers on the "AI traffic" conversation: ChatGPT drives roughly 92% of measured referral traffic from AI platforms. The same study confirmed AI referrals are still a small share of total traffic — but they're growing, and they behave differently from search visitors.

Designing pages for AI-referred visitors

An AI-referred visitor arrives pre-briefed. They've already asked the assistant to compare you against alternatives, and they land deep in your site — a pricing page, a case study, a service page — not your homepage. That has three design consequences:

  • Every page is a landing page. Deep pages need the same conversion furniture as your homepage: a clear value statement, proof, and one obvious next step.
  • Answer the comparison question on-page. If visitors arrive mid-evaluation, pages that address "why us, versus the obvious alternative" convert better than pages that assume a first-touch visitor.
  • Segment AI referrals in analytics now. Create a channel group for chatgpt.com, perplexity.ai, gemini.google.com and friends, so you have a baseline before this channel gets big.

3. Google's verdict on llms.txt: no ranking benefit

Site owners have spent months adding llms.txt and author.txt files hoping to influence AI visibility. This week Google said plainly: these files provide no search ranking benefit. That doesn't make them useless for other AI crawlers, but if an agency is selling you "llms.txt optimisation" as an SEO deliverable, you now have Google's own answer.

What actually influences AI visibility

The unglamorous fundamentals: quotable, well-structured content with clear heading hierarchy; direct answers positioned high in each section; real authorship signals on the page itself; structured data that machines can parse; and fast, crawlable HTML that isn't buried under JavaScript. Every one of those is a decision made during HTML web development — the unfashionable layer where markup, headings and page structure get built, which is why "SEO" increasingly can't be bolted on after launch.

4. Canonicalization takes up to two weeks — plan redesigns accordingly

A quieter but genuinely useful confirmation from Google: canonical changes — tags, redirects, internal link updates, duplicate-page cleanups — can take up to two weeks to fully process. If you've ever launched a website redesign and watched rankings wobble for a fortnight, this is why.

The migration checklist this confirms

  • Map every URL before launch. Old URL → new URL, one row each, no wildcard guessing for your top pages.
  • 301 everything that moved, update internal links to point at final destinations (not through redirect chains), and update the sitemap the same day.
  • Freeze for 14 days. Don't judge the relaunch's SEO impact before day 14, and never stack a second round of URL changes on top of an unprocessed first round.
  • Watch Search Console's page indexing report, not just rankings — it shows canonicalization catching up in near-real time.
What we'd do about it

Build the two-week window into every migration plan and every client conversation. A redesign that loses rankings usually didn't fail at design — it failed at redirect discipline. This is exactly why we treat SEO preservation as part of the development scope, not an afterthought.

5. Platform notes: WordPress 7.0.1, Search Console social properties, and a reviews scare

  • WordPress 7.0.1 shipped on 9 July — a pure bug-fix release bundling 13 core tickets and 13 Gutenberg fixes. Low-risk update; take it. The responsive-styling call for testing from 3 July also continued through this week, previewing per-viewport block styling coming in 7.1.
  • Search Console added properties for tracking how content on YouTube, Instagram, TikTok and other social platforms appears in Google Search — a sign Google increasingly sees your "site" as your whole footprint, not one domain. If social content is part of your funnel, you can now measure its search visibility properly.
  • Google Business Profile briefly showed "You have no reviews yet" on profiles with plenty of reviews. It was a display bug, not a removal — but it was a reminder of how much local conversion rests on a platform you don't control. Local intent searches — "web developers near me", "website design company near me" — lean heavily on profile signals like reviews, so a glitch there is a real business risk. Keep testimonials, case studies and proof on your own website too, where no glitch can hide them.
  • Google Ads now requires disclosure for AI-generated content depicting real people or events — relevant if your ad creative pipeline uses synthetic imagery.

What this week means for your website strategy

Three threads worth pulling together if you own a business website or are scoping a web design project:

  • Volatility is the weather now. The sites that ignore tremors are the ones built well: fast, structured, genuinely expert. Resilience to algorithm noise is a build-quality feature.
  • The AI channel is small but concentrated. One referrer dominates, visitors land deep, and the sites that convert them treat every page as a landing page.
  • There are no shortcuts files. llms.txt won't rank you. Structure, speed, authorship and quotable content will — and all four are decided when the site is designed and built. It's the sharpest question to put to any website development company you're evaluating: show me how the build itself earns rankings.

Frequently asked questions

What should I do when Google rankings suddenly drop?

Diagnose before acting: separate rank drops from click drops, compare competitors, rule out technical causes, and wait a full week before changing content. Panic edits during volatility routinely turn temporary dips into permanent losses.

Does an llms.txt file help SEO?

No — Google confirmed this week it provides no ranking benefit. Spend the effort on content structure, page speed and schema markup instead.

How long does Google take to process a website redesign?

Up to two weeks for canonicalization signals — redirects, canonical tags, internal links — to fully process. Plan the freeze window into your launch schedule.

How much traffic do AI chatbots actually send?

Still a small share of the total, but concentrated: ChatGPT accounts for roughly 92% of measured AI referrals. Baseline it now so you can see the curve as it grows.

Should I search for "web developers near me" or hire remotely?

Proximity matters less than process. What actually predicts a good outcome: timezone overlap for reviews and launches, evidence of fast Core Web Vitals on shipped work, and redirect discipline during migrations. Many businesses get better results from a specialised remote website development company than from whoever happens to be closest — judge the checklist, not the postcode.

The takeaway

The thread running through this week: the gap between what people think moves the needle (llms.txt files, panic edits during volatility) and what actually does (structure, speed, patience, and content worth quoting). Google is watching everything, confirming little, and rewarding websites that hold their nerve and their fundamentals.
