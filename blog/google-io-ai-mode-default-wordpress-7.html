Weekly Recap · Web Design, Dev & SEO

AI Mode goes default, a core update starts rolling, and WordPress 7.0 finally ships.

25 May 202610 min read

Some weeks the industry drips news. The week of 18–24 May 2026 fired all of it at once: Google I/O made AI Mode the default search experience worldwide, then Google launched the May 2026 core update two days later while everyone was still digesting the keynote. WordPress 7.0 "Armstrong" shipped the same day I/O ended, the web platform picked up an agent-facing standard called WebMCP, and Firefox 151 landed with tougher anti-fingerprinting. If you own a business website, at least three of these change decisions you'll make this quarter. Here's the recap — and what we'd do about each item.

1. Google I/O: AI Mode is now the default search experience

What happened

At Google I/O on 19 May, Google made AI Mode the default search experience globally, powered by the new Gemini 3.5 Flash model — the company's own framing was that Search has entered the "agentic Gemini era." The ten blue links are no longer the primary surface for over a billion users: a synthesized, conversational answer is the first thing searchers see. Google also rebuilt the search box for the first time in 25+ years — it now accepts long questions, images, files and open tabs as input.

Why it matters for your traffic

  • Zero-click goes up. Early analyst estimates put AI Mode's zero-click rate around 93% for queries it fully answers. The clicks that survive are higher intent — visitors arrive pre-briefed and comparison-ready.
  • Citation is the new ranking. If your brand isn't cited inside the AI response, you effectively don't exist for that query. Being position three on a page nobody scrolls to is worth very little.
  • Google's own guidance is blunt. Its Guide to Optimizing for Generative AI Features in Search (published 16 May, just before I/O) says optimizing for AI search "is still SEO" — and leans hard on one principle: create non-commodity content with first-hand experience and expert depth a model can't synthesize from elsewhere.
What we'd do about it

Stop measuring only rankings and start measuring citations and branded demand. Then audit whether machines can actually parse your site: real heading hierarchy, structured data, direct answers high on each page, and fast server-rendered HTML. Those are build decisions, which is why we'd put this question to any website development company before signing: show me how the build itself earns AI citations, not just how it looks.

2. The May 2026 core update started on 21 May

What happened

Two days after the AI Mode announcement, Google launched the May 2026 core update on 21 May, with a rollout expected to take about two weeks. Google calls it "a regular update designed to better surface relevant, satisfying content" — but tracking tools flagged elevated volatility across the 23 May weekend, and early industry chatter suggests this one is moving more than the March 2026 core update did.

The mid-rollout rules

  • Annotate 21 May in your analytics and Search Console so every future traffic conversation has the right reference line.
  • Don't react mid-rollout. Positions routinely swing one way and settle another before a core update completes. Any rewrite you make this fortnight is a decision made on incomplete data.
  • Log, don't edit. Keep a record of which queries and pages moved. When the completion announcement lands, you'll have a clean before/after instead of a contaminated one.
  • Separate the two events. With AI Mode going default the same week, a click drop isn't automatically the core update. Check whether positions moved or only clicks — they have different causes and different fixes.
What we'd do about it

Core updates reward the same things AI Mode does: genuine expertise, clean structure, fast pages. If a site gets hit by every core update, that's rarely a content problem alone — it's a fundamentals problem, and fundamentals are set during design and development. Hold your nerve for two weeks, then act on the settled data.

3. WordPress 7.0 "Armstrong" shipped on 20 May

After slipping from its original 9 April date, WordPress 7.0 — named Armstrong — was released on 20 May. It's a major architectural release: a refreshed admin design, new blocks, and a Web Client AI API. Notably, real-time co-editing was pulled late in the cycle over concerns about race conditions, server load and memory efficiency — a sensible call, and a signal of how much changed under the hood.

Our upgrade posture for business sites

  • Don't update production on day one. Major releases plus custom themes plus a long plugin stack is where launch-day surprises live.
  • Test on staging this week: theme rendering, checkout or lead forms, key plugin compatibility, and the new admin flows your team uses daily.
  • But do update within weeks, not months. Sitting on an old branch is a security posture, and a bad one.

4. The web platform gets agent-ready: WebMCP, partial updates and a smarter Baseline

The developer side of I/O mattered as much as the search side. Chrome's team shipped a slate of platform announcements that all point the same direction — the browser as a place where AI agents operate websites, not just people:

  • WebMCP — a proposed open standard that lets a site expose structured tools (JavaScript functions, HTML forms) that browser agents can call directly. The experimental origin trial starts in Chrome 149.
  • Declarative Partial Updates — new primitives for out-of-order HTML updates and streaming HTML into the page without heavy DOM manipulation, available for testing now.
  • Scroll-triggered animations — a declarative CSS alternative to IntersectionObserver for firing animations when a scroll boundary is crossed.
  • A new Baseline Checker that connects to your Google Analytics data and shows what percentage of your real users support modern features — turning "can we use this yet?" from a guess into a query.

The strategic read: agents consume the same markup crawlers do. Semantic, accessible html web development — proper forms, labelled controls, real buttons — is quietly becoming the interface layer for AI, which means accessibility work now pays twice. This applies to commerce too: agent-driven buying journeys will favour storefronts with clean structured product data, which is exactly the kind of build discipline good Shopify development partners bring to a store before the traffic arrives.

5. Browser and ads notes: Firefox 151 and ChatGPT Ads Manager

  • Firefox 151 shipped on 19 May with a redesigned Firefox Home (New Tab), stronger anti-fingerprinting in standard tracking protection, and an "End Private Session" button that wipes a private session without closing the window. The 152 beta (20 May) previews field-sizing — form controls that grow to fit their contents, a small but genuinely useful CSS win for form-heavy pages.
  • Stronger fingerprinting protection matters for marketers: it's another nudge away from third-party tracking and toward first-party data — email capture, logged-in experiences, and server-side measurement on your own domain.
  • OpenAI updated ChatGPT Ads Manager with daily budgets and geo-targeting down to US states, DMAs and ZIP codes. The AI-native ad channel is maturing fast; if your customers ask ChatGPT before they ask Google, this is now a testable line item.

What this week means for your website strategy

Pull the threads together and one theme emerges: the website is becoming an interface for machines that act on behalf of your customers. AI Mode summarizes you. Agents will soon operate you. Three decisions follow for anyone scoping a build this year:

  • Structure is strategy. Semantic markup, heading hierarchy, structured data and server-rendered speed decide whether AI Mode can cite you and whether agents can use you. They can't be sprinkled on after launch — they're the architecture.
  • Every surviving click is precious. If AI answers the easy queries, the visitors who do land are deep in evaluation. That makes conversion optimization on service pages, pricing pages and case studies the highest-leverage work on the site — every page needs proof and one obvious next step.
  • Own your demand. Fingerprinting crackdowns, zero-click answers, platform ad channels — every trend this week pushes toward assets you control: your list, your content, your site's ability to convert. Whether you search for "web developers near me" or brief a specialist remotely, judge candidates on exactly this: do they build sites that hold up when the click is scarce?

Frequently asked questions

What does AI Mode becoming Google's default mean for my website?

Fewer generic clicks, but higher-intent ones. AI Mode answers many queries directly, so visibility now means being cited inside the AI response. Google's own guidance says optimizing for generative AI search is still SEO: non-commodity content, first-hand expertise, clean structure, fast crawlable HTML, and structured data. Sites that were built well don't need a new playbook — they need to double down on it.

The May 2026 core update started on 21 May — my rankings moved. What should I do?

Nothing yet. The rollout is expected to take about two weeks, and positions routinely swing mid-rollout before settling. Annotate 21 May in your analytics, keep records of what moved, and wait for the completion announcement before changing content. Rewrites made mid-rollout are decisions made on incomplete data.

Should I update to WordPress 7.0 right away?

Not on day one for a business-critical site. WordPress 7.0 is a major architectural release with a refreshed admin design, and real-time collaboration was pulled late in the cycle — a sign of how much changed under the hood. Test on staging first, confirm your theme and key plugins are compatible, then update within a few weeks so you're not stranded on an old branch.

What is WebMCP and should my business care?

WebMCP is a proposed open standard, announced at Google I/O 2026, that lets a website expose structured tools — functions and forms — that browser AI agents can operate directly. It's in origin trial from Chrome 149. Most businesses shouldn't build for it yet, but it confirms the direction: sites will increasingly be operated by agents as well as read by people, which rewards clean semantic HTML and well-structured forms. For ecommerce stores, ask your Shopify developer partner where structured data and agent-readiness sit on the roadmap.

Did anything this week change how a website should actually be built?

Yes — the bar moved toward structure and speed. AI Mode and browser agents both consume the underlying HTML, not the pixels, so semantic markup, real heading hierarchy, structured data and fast server-rendered pages now decide whether machines can even represent you. Those are build-time decisions; retrofitting them after launch costs far more than getting them right in development.

The takeaway

In one week, Google changed what search is, then changed how it ranks what's left. The businesses that come out of this fortnight ahead won't be the ones with the cleverest hack — they'll be the ones whose sites were built as structured, fast, genuinely expert assets that machines can cite and customers can act on. Everything announced this week rewards that build quality; nothing announced this week replaces it.
