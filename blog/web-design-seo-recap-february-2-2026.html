Weekly Recap · Web Design, Dev & SEO

Heated volatility, AI Overviews that keep talking, and a possible opt-out from AI search.

2 February 202610 min read

January went out swinging. In the week of 26 January to 1 February, rank trackers ran hot with no confirmed Google update to blame, AI Overviews learned to take follow-up questions and pull searchers straight into AI Mode, and reports surfaced that Google is testing a way for websites to opt out of AI search features entirely. Add Yahoo's new AI search assistant, a Chrome stable security update, a WordPress tooling release and a local-SEO fix, and it was a dense week for anyone whose website is how customers find and choose them. Here's what happened — and, for each item, whether it actually moves revenue for a business website.

1. Late-January ranking volatility: heated, sustained, unconfirmed

What happened

Through the back half of January, rank-tracking tools showed elevated, sustained volatility, and site owners reported meaningful traffic swings in both directions — with no confirmed update from Google. Search Engine Roundtable, which has tracked these tremors for years, logged the movement as "heated." The consensus read: continuous algorithm adjustments plus the ongoing integration of AI features are reshaping results between named updates, not just during them.

The triage sequence when rankings move and Google says nothing

  • Day 1–2: diagnose, don't act. In Search Console, separate position changes from click changes. A rank drop and a click drop have different causes — and the second is increasingly an AI-features story, not a penalty story.
  • Check the query class. Informational queries losing clicks while positions hold usually means an AI Overview expanded above you. Commercial queries moving usually means competitive churn.
  • Compare competitors. If everyone in your niche moved, it's the algorithm breathing. If only you moved, audit what changed on your site in the last 30 days.
  • Rule out the boring causes. Broken redirects, a slow deploy, deindexed pages, an expired plugin — technical faults explain more "algorithm hits" than algorithms do.
  • Day 7+: act on evidence, not on a single scary Monday of data.
What we'd do about it

Volatility without a named update is now the default weather, and the sites that shrug it off share the same traits: fast pages, clean structure, real expertise, and conversion paths that work no matter which page a visitor lands on. If your site gets whiplash from every tremor, that's a build-quality problem before it's a content problem — the kind a website development company should be solving at the architecture level, not patching with blog posts.

2. AI Overviews now take follow-up questions — straight into AI Mode

Google rolled out the ability to ask follow-up questions inside an AI Overview, with the conversation jumping directly into AI Mode — no new search, no visit to a website in between. Search Engine Land's blunt framing: people spend more time in AI-driven answers and less on actual site links.

What this means for your pages

  • Informational traffic will keep thinning. The query that used to send a top-of-funnel visitor to your blog may now resolve entirely inside a conversation with Google.
  • Citations are the new impressions. If the journey happens inside AI Mode, being the source it quotes — and links — is the visibility that's left. That rewards quotable, well-structured sections with direct answers placed high.
  • The clicks that survive are worth more. A visitor who leaves an AI conversation for your site has already been briefed and is deep in evaluation. Every landing page needs proof, pricing clarity and one obvious next step — this is conversion optimization work, and it's no longer optional.
What we'd do about it

Stop measuring content by sessions and start measuring it by qualified actions. Restructure key pages so each section answers one question directly enough to be cited, then make sure the page converts the smaller, hotter stream of clicks that still arrives. Fewer visits at a higher close rate can be a revenue upgrade — but only if the pages are built for it.

3. Google tests letting sites opt out of AI search features

Reporting this week indicated Google is testing opt-out controls that would let websites decline inclusion in AI-driven summaries like AI Overviews — a response to sustained publisher and regulator pressure over how content gets used in AI answers.

Think twice before celebrating

An opt-out sounds like leverage, but for most business websites it's a trap. If your service pages leave the AI answer while your competitors' stay in, you haven't protected your content — you've vacated the shelf. The calculation differs by content type: paywalled editorial and premium research may rationally opt out; commercial pages almost never should. Until the mechanics ship, the right move is to watch, not pledge.

What we'd do about it

Decide per content type now, so you're not improvising when the control arrives. Keep commercial and service content opted in and optimised for citation; reserve any opt-out for content whose value depends on exclusivity. And keep building the asset no platform controls: an email list and a site that converts direct traffic.

4. Yahoo Scout joins the AI assistant pile-up

Yahoo launched Yahoo Scout, a generative AI search assistant woven into Yahoo Search, Mail, News and Finance. Notably, Yahoo positioned it as designed to drive clicks to websites — a deliberate contrast with answer engines that keep users inside the box. Two related notes from the same week sharpen the picture: Google DeepMind's CEO confirmed Gemini will stay ad-free for now to protect user trust, and OpenAI's Sam Altman admitted GPT-5.2 sacrificed writing quality in favour of reasoning and coding.

The practical takeaway isn't "optimise for Yahoo." It's that the assistant landscape is fragmenting, and the sites that win across all of them do the same unglamorous things: clean heading hierarchy, direct answers, structured data, and fast crawlable HTML. That last one matters more than it sounds — html web development choices made at build time decide whether an assistant can parse your pages at all. And Altman's admission is a quiet warning for anyone publishing raw AI output: if the model's own maker says the writing got worse, undifferentiated AI content is a weaker bet than ever.

5. Platform notes: Chrome security update, WordPress testing tooling, and a local SEO fix

  • Chrome shipped a stable channel update for desktop on 27 January, followed by early stable, iOS and Android updates on the 28th. As ever with stable channel releases, security fixes ride along — update your browsers, and smoke-test your site in the new build if you rely on newer CSS or JavaScript features.
  • WordPress got a new AI agent skill, wp-playground, on 30 January. Built on the Playground CLI, it gives developers a fast, repeatable way to spin up WordPress environments for automated plugin and theme testing. If AI agents are part of your development workflow, this closes a real speed gap in WordPress QA.
  • Google Business Profile review appeals unclogged. Google removed the messaging warning of delayed review appeals, suggesting the backlog is resolved. If you had a legitimate review removal appeal stuck in limbo, resubmit it now — for local businesses, the review panel is often the highest-converting pixel Google shows.
  • Google published research on compact intent-prediction models that can anticipate what a user wants before they finish typing, running on-device. It's a research signal, not a shipped feature — but it points at a future where the query box itself is an AI surface.

What this week means for your website strategy

Three threads worth pulling together if you're scoping a build, a replatform or a redesign this quarter:

  • Design for the post-click, not the click. AI Overviews' follow-up feature confirms the direction: fewer, hotter visits. The sites that grow revenue in that world treat every deep page as a landing page and every landing page as a conversion asset.
  • Build quality is algorithm insurance. A week of heated volatility with no confirmed update is the strongest argument yet for fundamentals — speed, structure, semantic markup — being decided in the build, not bolted on after.
  • Platform choices are AI-visibility choices. On commerce builds this cuts sharply: a store's theme architecture decides how parseable your product data is to assistants. It's why we'd put "show me how the build earns citations" on the evaluation checklist for any Shopify development partners you're considering — a good shopify developer partner treats structured data and rendering speed as launch criteria, not post-launch tickets.

Frequently asked questions

Why did Google rankings move so much in late January 2026?

Rank-tracking tools showed heated, sustained volatility through the week of 26 January with no confirmed update from Google. The movement is widely attributed to continuous algorithm adjustments and the ongoing integration of AI features into results. Diagnose before acting: separate rank drops from click drops, compare competitors on the same queries, rule out technical causes, and wait a full week of data before rewriting anything.

What do AI Overviews follow-up questions mean for my website traffic?

Google now lets searchers ask follow-up questions inside an AI Overview and jump straight into AI Mode without ever visiting a website. Expect more zero-click journeys on informational queries. The defence is to earn citations by structuring content into quotable, directly-answered sections, and to make every page that still gets a click convert harder.

Should my site opt out of Google's AI search features if the option ships?

Probably not by default. Google is only testing opt-out controls for AI-driven summaries, and opting out likely trades visibility for principle: your competitors stay in the answer while you disappear from it. Wait for the mechanics, then decide per content type — commercial pages usually benefit from inclusion, while paywalled or premium editorial may not.

Is Yahoo Scout something I should optimise for?

Not specifically. Yahoo Scout is a generative AI search assistant across Yahoo Search, Mail, News and Finance, and Yahoo says it is designed to drive clicks to websites. The optimisation work is the same as for every AI surface: clean structure, direct answers, fast crawlable HTML and real authority signals. Do that once and every assistant can cite you.

Do I need to act on this week's Chrome and WordPress updates?

Yes on Chrome — the 27 January stable channel update includes security fixes, so test your site in the new build and keep browsers current. The WordPress news is tooling, not core: a new wp-playground AI agent skill (30 January) makes automated plugin and theme testing faster, which is worth adopting in your development workflow rather than something to patch on production.

The takeaway

The week's pattern is hard to miss: search is becoming a conversation, and the click is becoming the exception rather than the rule. What survives the shift is unchanged from every previous shift — websites built fast, structured cleanly, and designed to convert whoever still arrives. Whether you search for "web developers near me" or brief an agency across the world, that's the bar to hold them to: not whether the site looks good on launch day, but whether it earns citations, absorbs volatility, and turns fewer visits into more revenue.
