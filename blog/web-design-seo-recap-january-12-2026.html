Weekly Recap · Web Design, Dev & SEO

Post-core aftershocks, a 6 January volatility spike, and CES 2026's AI hardware wave.

12 January 202610 min read

The December 2025 core update finished on 29 December — and the SERPs never actually settled down. Between 5 and 11 January the SEO world watched an unconfirmed volatility spike hit on 6 January, digested Google's January webmaster report on what the core update actually rewarded, and spotted Google quietly personalizing AI Overviews and AI Mode answers. Meanwhile CES 2026 filled Las Vegas with AI PCs and agentic hardware, and the WordPress project laid out an ambitious 2026. If your website is how customers find and choose you, here's the week in full — and what we'd actually do about each item.

1. The 6 January volatility spike: aftershock, not update

What happened

On Tuesday 6 January, SEOs and site owners started reporting large drops in Google rankings — enough chatter that Search Engine Roundtable covered it as an unconfirmed update. Google confirmed nothing. Curiously, most rank-tracking tools stayed relatively calm while forum complaints grew louder, which usually points at a narrower shakeup: certain site types and query classes moving hard while the broad index barely breathes.

Context matters here. The December 2025 core update ran from 11 to 29 December, and this 6 January tremor looks like the first aftershock of Google recalibrating after it — the same post-core pattern the industry has seen after every recent core update.

The triage sequence when rankings move and Google says nothing

  • Day 1–2: diagnose, don't act. Separate rank changes from click changes in Search Console — a position drop and a click drop have different causes and different fixes.
  • Check the query class. Losing brand queries is rare and alarming; losing competitive commercial queries is churn; losing informational queries is often AI answer expansion, not a penalty.
  • Compare competitors on the same queries. If everyone moved, it's the algorithm breathing. If only you moved, review what changed on your site in the last 30 days.
  • Rule out technical causes. A slow deploy, broken redirects, an expired plugin — boring causes explain more "algorithm hits" than algorithms do.
  • Day 7+: act on evidence, once a full week of data shows a sustained pattern.
What we'd do about it

Nothing panicked. Post-core aftershocks routinely round-trip within days on sites with strong fundamentals — fast pages, clean structure, real expertise. If your site flinches at every tremor, that's the tell that the foundation needs work, and that's a website design and development problem before it's a content problem.

2. The December core update post-mortem: specialists won

With the update fully rolled out, the first wave of aftermath analysis landed this week alongside Google's January 2026 webmaster report. The through-lines across the data studies:

  • Specialists beat generalists. Sites with demonstrated first-hand experience and topical depth gained; broad, thin, everything-sites lost.
  • Affiliate and YMYL sites took the biggest swings — one industry analysis put affiliate impact at roughly 71% of tracked sites, health/YMYL at 67%, and e-commerce at 52%.
  • Recovery is a project, not a patch. The analyses converge on 50–80% traffic recovery within 3–6 months of systematic content and technical work, with full recovery often taking 6–12 months.

For e-commerce owners the 52% figure deserves attention. Product and collection pages that are thin templates wrapped around a title tag were exactly the kind of page this update discounted. It's why we tell store owners evaluating Shopify development partners to ask about content architecture — unique buying guidance, real specs, structured data on every template — not just theme speed. A good shopify developer partner treats the product page as a piece of content, not a database row.

What we'd do about it

If the December update hit you, resist the urge to delete or mass-rewrite in January. Map your money pages, score each one honestly against "would an expert publish this?", and rebuild the weakest 20% properly. Slow, systematic quality work is the only recovery path the data supports.

3. Google is quietly personalizing AI Overviews and AI Mode

What happened

Also covered in this week's search news cycle: Google was spotted personalizing some AI Overviews and AI Mode responses, tailoring answers to the searcher. In the same week, users noticed Google Discover surfacing an unusual number of posts from X — another reminder of how fast Google's surfaces now shift.

Why personalization changes your measurement

  • "What do we rank for?" gets fuzzier. Two people searching the same query can see different AI answers citing different sources. Average position was already a blunt metric; personalization dulls it further.
  • Measure outcomes, not positions. Track clicks, leads and revenue by landing page. That data is yours, unaffected by whose SERP you're looking at.
  • Be the citable source. Personalized or not, AI answers still pull from clearly structured, quotable pages. Direct answers high in each section, clean heading hierarchy, and schema markup remain the entry ticket.

4. CES 2026: the AI hardware wave reaches the browser

CES 2026 ran 6–9 January in Las Vegas, and the show belonged to AI: NVIDIA's Vera Rubin architecture, the DGX Spark desktop with a GB10 Superchip and 128 GB of unified memory for running models locally, and a flood of AI PCs from Intel, AMD and Qualcomm with on-device NPUs. The recurring theme was AI moving from chat windows into proactive agents and physical devices.

What a hardware show means for websites

More capable local AI means more visits from assistants and agents that read, summarise and compare websites on a user's behalf — before the human ever clicks. That raises the stakes on the unfashionable layer of html web development: semantic markup, real heading structure, server-rendered content that doesn't require a JavaScript gauntlet to parse, and structured data that states your prices, services and locations in machine-readable form. Agents can't be charmed by animations. They read the markup, and they move on.

What we'd do about it

Run an "agent readability" pass on your key pages: disable JavaScript and check what's actually in the HTML. If your value proposition, pricing and proof aren't there, they're invisible to a growing class of visitors. Then make sure every page a human lands on still converts — that's conversion optimization work, and it compounds with everything above.

5. Platform notes: WordPress's ambitious 2026

  • Three major releases return. The WordPress project confirmed a return to three major releases in 2026, with planning for WordPress 7.0 under way and a call for volunteers announced. If your site runs WordPress, budget for more frequent update-and-test cycles this year.
  • Gutenberg 22.3 — covered in this month's WordPress developer roundup — added a dedicated Appearance → Fonts management screen, let the Grid block use minimum column width and column count together, and completed PHP-only block registration with full block metadata support.
  • SPA-like editing is coming. Recent routing work moves Gutenberg toward app-like navigation across the block and site editors — a signal of where WordPress's editing experience is heading in 7.0.

What this week means for your website strategy

Three threads worth pulling together if you own a business website or are scoping a redesign for 2026:

  • Volatility is the baseline now. An unconfirmed spike a week after a core update finished is normal. Sites built well — fast, structured, genuinely expert — ride tremors; thin sites absorb them. Resilience is a build-quality feature.
  • Depth beats breadth. December's winners were specialists. The question to put to any website development company you're evaluating in 2026: show me how the build itself demonstrates expertise — templates, schema, content architecture — not just how it looks.
  • Design for two readers. Every page now serves a human deciding whether to buy and a machine deciding whether to cite you. The sites that win this year satisfy both — and that's decided at build time, not bolted on after. Searching "web developers near me" and picking the closest office won't get you there; judging the checklist will.

Frequently asked questions

Was there a Google update on 6 January 2026?

Nothing confirmed. SEOs reported significant ranking drops on 6 January, but Google stayed silent, and with the December core update finishing on 29 December the likeliest explanation is post-core recalibration. Diagnose before acting, and give it a full week of data before touching content.

How long does recovery from the December 2025 core update take?

The industry analyses published this week suggest 50–80% traffic recovery within 3–6 months of systematic improvement, and often 6–12 months for full recovery. There's no shortcut — the update rewarded demonstrated experience and depth, and those take real work to build.

What do personalized AI Overviews mean for my traffic?

Rank tracking becomes less reliable as a health metric, because two searchers can see different answers citing different sources. Shift your reporting to clicks, leads and revenue by landing page — outcomes you can measure regardless of whose SERP is on screen.

Does CES 2026 matter for a business website?

Indirectly but genuinely. The AI PC and agent wave means more of your "visitors" will be machines reading your markup on a human's behalf. Clean semantic HTML, server-rendered content and structured data determine whether those agents represent you accurately.

What's coming in WordPress in 2026?

Three major releases instead of two, with WordPress 7.0 planning already under way, plus Gutenberg's new Fonts screen, improved Grid block and full PHP-only block registration. Practically: more frequent updates to test, and a steadily more app-like editing experience.

The takeaway

The first full working week of 2026 set the year's tone: Google confirms little and recalibrates constantly, AI answers are getting personal, and the hardware world just committed billions to putting agents between your customers and your website. The response to all three is the same unglamorous work — structure, speed, depth and proof, built into the site rather than sprinkled on top. That's what holds its nerve when the SERPs don't.
