Weekly Recap · Web Design, Dev & SEO

End-of-April volatility, preferred sources go global, and contrast-color() hits Baseline.

4 May 202610 min read

The March core update officially wrapped on 8 April — and three weeks later, the trackers lit up again anyway. Between 27 April and 3 May the SEO world watched an unconfirmed volatility spike, Google quietly gave every signed-in user worldwide a "show me more of this site" button, Alphabet's Q1 numbers confirmed the open-web ad layer keeps shrinking, and the web platform closed out a genuinely good month for CSS. Meanwhile WordPress sites got a firm new date for 7.0. If your website is how customers find and choose you, here's the week — and what we'd actually do about each item.

1. Ranking volatility heats up as April closes — with no confirmed update

What happened

Around 27–28 April, Search Engine Roundtable reported new signs of Google ranking volatility: rank-tracking tools trended upward and forum chatter spiked over a 24-hour window, even though some tools stayed calm. Google confirmed nothing. Context matters here — the March 2026 core update started 27 March and completed 8 April, and this end-of-April tremor followed an earlier flare-up around 23 April. The pattern of 2026 so far: the algorithm keeps moving between named updates, not just during them.

How to read an unconfirmed tremor

  • Compare time windows, not days. Look at 7, 14 and 28-day views in Search Console before concluding anything. One bad Tuesday is weather, not climate.
  • Separate rankings from clicks. A position drop and a click drop have different causes — and increasingly, stable rankings with falling clicks points at AI surfaces, not the algorithm.
  • Check the neighbourhood. If competitors on your money queries moved too, it's redistribution. If only you moved, audit what changed on your site in the last 30 days.
  • Rule out the boring stuff. Broken redirects, a slow deploy, deindexed pages and expired plugins explain more "algorithm hits" than algorithms do.
What we'd do about it

Nothing dramatic — and that's the point. Two flare-ups in a week with no confirmation is exactly when panic edits do the most damage. Sites with strong fundamentals — fast pages, clean structure, real expertise — tend to round-trip through these tremors. If your site flinches at every one, that's a build-quality signal, and it's a website design and development conversation before it's a content one.

2. Preferred sources roll out globally: your site can now be chosen

On 30 April, Google's preferred sources feature rolled out globally across all supported languages. Signed-in users can mark sites they want to see more of, and Google leans toward those sites in their results. It started as a US/India experiment; now it's everywhere.

Why this is bigger than it looks

For years, SEO has been about winning an algorithm's estimate of what a stranger wants. Preferred sources adds a second track: winning an actual human's deliberate choice. That shifts value toward things algorithms only approximate — brand memory, repeat visits, a site people want back in their results.

  • Brand queries are the leading indicator. If people search your name, they'll star your site. If they only ever find you generically, they won't.
  • Give people a reason to return. A weekly insights page, genuinely useful tools, updated pricing — sites that reward a second visit earn the star.
  • Design for recognition. A distinctive, professional site is now literally a ranking asset for everyone who prefers it. Template sameness has a new cost.
What we'd do about it

Treat preferred sources as a retention metric for search. The same work that improves conversion optimization — clarity, speed, trust signals, a reason to come back — is now the work that gets your site chosen. Ask a simple question of every page: would a customer star this?

3. Alphabet's Q1: the open-web ad layer keeps shrinking

Alphabet reported Q1 2026 earnings on 29 April. The line that matters for website owners: Google Network ad revenue fell 4% year over year to $6.97 billion — the segment that pays out to third-party sites running Google's display ads. Google's own properties and AI surfaces keep growing; the syndicated open-web layer keeps contracting.

If part of your traffic or revenue model leans on the open display web — as an advertiser buying it or a publisher selling it — the trend line is the message. Rented reach is getting less reliable and more expensive per real visitor, which quietly raises the value of everything you own: your website, your search visibility, your email list, and pages built to convert the traffic you've already earned.

4. The web platform's April, in review: contrast-color() goes Baseline

As the month closed, web.dev published its April round-up of what shipped in browsers. It was a strong month: Chrome 147 and Firefox 150 both reached stable (no stable Safari release in April), and several CSS features crossed into interoperability.

What's newly usable

  • contrast-color() is Baseline Newly available. With Chrome 147 shipping it, the function now works across all major engines: pass it a colour and it returns black or white, whichever contrasts most. Themed buttons, badges and banners keep readable text automatically — an accessibility win you previously had to hand-roll.
  • Scroll-driven animation range properties reached Baseline — scroll-linked effects with no JavaScript scroll listeners, which means no jank tax.
  • CSS border-shape landed for non-rectangular borders, and modulepreload now covers JSON and CSS modules.
  • Firefox 150 (21 April) also brought colour tooling for developers: color-mix() now accepts any number of colours and light-dark() accepts image values — plus 359 security fixes, so update.
  • In beta: container style queries in Firefox 151, and the :open pseudo-class for details, dialog and select elements in Safari 26.5.
What we'd do about it

Features like contrast-color() and scroll-driven animations replace JavaScript with CSS — which means faster pages, and faster pages move revenue. This is the argument for disciplined html web development over plugin-stacked builds: when the platform ships a capability, a clean codebase adopts it in an afternoon. It's a fair test for any website development company you're evaluating: ask what they've shipped with the last year of CSS.

5. WordPress 7.0 gets its date: 20 May, and a PHP deadline

After the unprecedented step back from Release Candidate to beta — taken to stabilise the real-time collaboration architecture — WordPress 7.0 "Armstrong" was rescheduled for 20 May 2026. This week sat squarely in the run-up, and the prep list is concrete:

  • PHP 7.2 and 7.3 support is dropped. The new minimum is PHP 7.4. If your host or a legacy plugin pins you to an older version, you have two weeks to fix it.
  • AI lands in core. The WP AI Client (a standard PHP interface to AI providers), the Connectors API for credentials, and a client-side Abilities API all ship — with official OpenAI, Google and Anthropic plugins already in the directory.
  • Test on staging now, especially heavily customised editors and older page builders. A first-point-oh release plus a platform-level architecture change is not the moment to auto-update production on day one.

What this week means for your website strategy

Three threads worth pulling together if you own a business website or are scoping a build this quarter:

  • Search is adding a human vote. Unconfirmed volatility you can't control; being worth starring as a preferred source you can. Brand, design quality and return-visit value are now literal ranking inputs.
  • Owned beats rented, again. Alphabet's Network decline is one more data point that syndicated reach is decaying. The compounding assets are your site, its rankings and its conversion paths.
  • Platform choice is a capability choice. Whether it's adopting new Baseline CSS or navigating a WordPress major release, execution quality decides outcomes. The same logic applies in ecommerce: merchants get further with experienced Shopify development partners than with generic builds, because a good shopify developer partner knows which platform changes to adopt and which to skip. Judge every vendor on that editorial sense, not their portfolio gloss.

The takeaway
