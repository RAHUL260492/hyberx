Shopify vs WordPress vs custom: an honest guide for 2026.

16 June 20269 min read

Ask three agencies which platform to build on and you’ll get three answers that suspiciously match what each agency sells. So here’s the version with the incentives removed: where Shopify genuinely wins, where WordPress and WooCommerce earn their complexity, when a custom build is actually justified — and the switching costs that don’t show up in the sales pitch.

The short version

  • Selling products is the business? Shopify, almost every time.
  • Content is the business, commerce is a side door? WordPress with WooCommerce.
  • The website is the product, or commerce logic is genuinely unusual? Custom — with eyes open about cost.

The long version is about the exceptions, because that’s where expensive mistakes live.

Where Shopify wins

Shopify’s real product isn’t the storefront — it’s everything behind it: PCI-compliant checkout that converts measurably better than most self-hosted attempts, payments, fraud analysis, inventory, tax handling, and an upgrade path that doesn’t break your store every quarter. In 2026, with checkout extensibility mature and Online Store 2.0 sections everywhere, the old “Shopify is inflexible” complaint is mostly dead. For a product business, the platform tax buys you an engineering team you don’t have to hire. That’s why our default for e-commerce is Shopify development — the honest answer is that most stores don’t have problems Shopify can’t solve.

Where it genuinely hurts: deep content and editorial (the blog is serviceable, not great), truly unusual catalogue logic (complex B2B pricing matrices, configurators), and transaction fees if you refuse Shopify Payments.

Where WordPress + WooCommerce earns its keep

If your growth engine is content — hundreds of articles, complex taxonomies, multi-author workflows — WordPress remains the strongest publishing platform ever built, and WooCommerce bolts commerce onto it credibly. You own everything, extend anything, and pay no platform rent.

The bill arrives as responsibility: hosting, security, updates, backups, and a plugin ecosystem where quality varies wildly. WooCommerce stores need genuine maintenance budgets — the “free” platform costs more in upkeep than Shopify costs in subscription for most small teams. And checkout conversion, out of the box, trails Shopify’s.

When custom is justified

A custom build — headless commerce, bespoke backend, your own stack — makes sense in exactly three situations: the website is the product (marketplaces, SaaS, booking platforms), commerce logic no platform models (unusual fulfilment, regulated industries), or scale where platform fees exceed an engineering team’s cost. Below that bar, custom means paying to rebuild solved problems — checkout, tax, fraud, PCI — and owning every bug yourself. Most brands asking for custom actually need a well-built platform store with two custom integrations.

What we’d do about it

Choose on your team’s shape, not feature checklists. No technical staff? Shopify — the constraint that protects you. A content team publishing daily? WordPress. In-house engineers and a product roadmap? Then, and only then, discuss custom. The right platform is the one whose failure modes your team can live with.

The switching costs nobody mentions

Migrations cost more than builds: URL redirects that must be perfect or rankings evaporate, order-history imports, app-feature parity you took for granted, and staff retraining. Budget a migration at 1.5x a fresh build and be pleasantly surprised. This is also why the platform decision deserves more care than most brands give it — done right, with clean website design and development foundations, you shouldn’t revisit it for five years or more.

Frequently asked questions

Is Shopify better than WordPress for e-commerce?

For most product-led businesses, yes — checkout, hosting, security and upgrades are handled, and Shopify’s checkout typically converts better out of the box. WooCommerce wins when content is the growth engine and the team can carry self-hosted maintenance.

How much does a custom e-commerce build cost compared to Shopify?

Typically 5–15x a professional Shopify build upfront, plus permanent engineering maintenance. Justified when the website is the product or the logic fits no platform — not for aesthetics.

Can I migrate from WooCommerce to Shopify without losing SEO?

Yes, with discipline: complete 301 redirect mapping, content parity on key pages, and Search Console re-verification. Rankings recover in weeks when redirects are perfect — and suffer for months when they’re not.

Do I need a Shopify Plus plan?

Only at real scale — Plus earns its price via checkout extensibility, lower rates and B2B features once you’re doing roughly ₹8–10 crore+ annually. Below that, standard Shopify with good development covers almost everything.

The takeaway

Shopify for product businesses, WordPress for content businesses, custom for when the website is the product — and honest accounting for the exceptions. Pick based on your team’s shape and the failure modes you can live with, budget migrations at 1.5x a fresh build, and make the decision once per half-decade instead of once per redesign.
