Weekly Recap · Web Design, Dev & SEO

The core update turns up the heat, ads land in AI Mode, and WordPress 7.0 meets the real world.

1 June 202610 min read

The May 2026 core update spent the week grinding through the index — then hit its loudest volatility spike yet over the weekend of 29–30 May, and it still isn't finished. Meanwhile the industry spent the week digesting Google Marketing Live's biggest bombshell: ads are coming to AI Mode, which just crossed a billion monthly users. Add Google's first official AI search optimisation guide, a native AI Assistant channel rolling out in GA4, WordPress 7.0's bumpy first fortnight, and a genuinely useful batch of CSS in Chrome 149 beta — and 25–31 May was one of the densest weeks of the year for anyone who owns a business website. Here's what happened, and what each item means for revenue.

1. The May 2026 core update's weekend spike — and it's not done yet

What happened

Google's May 2026 broad core update, which began rolling out on 21 May, was supposed to be settling. Instead, rank trackers and site owner reports showed volatility building from Friday 29 May and hitting hard on Saturday 30 May — the biggest single spike of the rollout so far, per Search Engine Roundtable's tracking. Early analysis from the SEO community suggests this update is landing heavier than March's, with visible impact across verticals and countries, and particular turbulence in YMYL niches. As of publication, Google has not confirmed completion.

The early pattern in who's winning

  • Topical depth over volume. Sites with fewer, deeply researched pages on a narrow topic are outperforming high-output content farms in early winner/loser analyses.
  • First-hand experience is decisive. Pages with real examples, original testing and case studies are gaining ground over generic rewrites and AI-scaled summaries.
  • Weekend spikes mean mid-rollout swings. Positions gained or lost on 30 May can reverse before the rollout completes — which is exactly why you don't react to them yet.
What we'd do about it

Nothing structural — yet. Mid-rollout is the worst possible time to rewrite pages, because you're reacting to a moving target. Log what moved and on which queries, rule out technical causes, and wait for Google to confirm completion plus a week of stable data. Then act on the pattern, not the panic. If every core update knocks your site around, the fix is usually build quality — speed, structure, genuine expertise on the page — which is a conversation for a website development company, not a content mill.

2. Ads are coming to AI Mode — with a billion users watching

What happened

Google Marketing Live ran on 20 May, but the last week of May was when the implications sank in. The headline: AI Mode has passed one billion monthly active users, with queries more than doubling every quarter — and Google will now place ads inside the conversational interface, formatted as native-looking conversational responses with a "Sponsored" tag, generated by Gemini for long-tail queries. Alongside that: an AI Max for Shopping one-click upgrade for retail campaigns, an agentic shopping cart that follows users across Search, Gemini and YouTube, and a Gemini-built "Ask Advisor" agent that spans Ads, Analytics and Merchant Center.

Why this matters for your website

  • The answer surface is now a paid surface. Some queries that used to resolve organically will show sponsored conversational answers first. Organic citations still appear — but the competition for attention inside the answer just got real.
  • Ecommerce is moving to agentic journeys. If shoppers can carry a cart across Search, Gemini and YouTube, your product data, feeds and storefront structure have to be machine-legible end to end. This is where working with experienced Shopify development partners pays off: clean product schema, fast storefronts and disciplined feeds are what agentic shopping runs on.
  • Landing page quality decides the economics. Paid clicks from AI Mode will cost real money. Whether they turn into revenue is a conversion optimization question, not a bidding question.
What we'd do about it

Treat this as a two-front build. Front one: make your pages the kind AI Mode cites — quotable, structured, fast. Front two: if you sell online, audit your product data now, before agentic checkout journeys harden into habits. A shopify developer partner who understands both feeds and page speed is worth more this year than another point of ad budget.

3. Google's official AI search guide: GEO is just SEO done well

Google's first official guide to optimising for generative AI features in Search — published mid-May and dissected across the industry all week — is quietly the most useful document of the month. Its core message: there is no separate discipline for AI Overviews and AI Mode. No secret files, no new markup, no "GEO" or "AEO" playbook. What gets cited is what already ranks well: crawlable, fast HTML; clear heading structure; unique, first-hand content that answers questions people can't find answered elsewhere.

The practical checklist hiding in the guide

  • Crawlability first. If Googlebot can't render it cheaply, AI features can't cite it. Content buried behind heavy JavaScript remains the most common self-inflicted wound we see in audits — and it's decided at the html web development layer, when the markup is written.
  • Structure is the interface. Clear h2/h3 hierarchy, direct answers high in each section, and schema where it genuinely applies.
  • Non-commodity content. Google's phrasing is blunt: unique perspective, original experience, answers that are hard to find elsewhere. Volume without that is now a liability, as the core update is demonstrating in real time.

4. GA4's AI Assistant channel starts rolling out

Google Analytics added a native AI Assistant channel to the Default Channel Group — announced 13 May, reaching more properties through late May. Visits from recognised assistants like ChatGPT, Gemini and Claude are automatically assigned an ai-assistant medium and grouped under the new channel, with no regex or developer work needed. Two caveats worth knowing: coverage is expanding property by property, and Perplexity isn't in the channel yet — it still lands in Referral.

What to do this week

  • Check whether your GA4 property has the channel yet, and annotate the date it appears — your channel-mix trends will shift when it does.
  • Keep (or build) a manual referral segment for AI sources not yet covered, so the "AI traffic" line in your reports is complete rather than just convenient.
  • Baseline conversion rate for the channel from day one. AI-referred visitors land deep and arrive pre-briefed; if they convert worse than search, that's a landing page problem worth fixing early.

5. WordPress 7.0's first fortnight: real upgrade, real friction

WordPress 7.0 shipped on 20 May, and the last week of May was when real sites started upgrading in numbers. The release brings AI foundations into core (an AI Client and Abilities API), a modernised admin, a Command Palette, and raised PHP requirements. Notably, real-time collaboration was pulled from the release late in the cycle over stability concerns — a sign the team chose shipping solid over shipping everything.

Our upgrade guidance

  • Don't upgrade a revenue site blind. Raised PHP requirements plus an admin rebuild means plugin and theme conflicts surface in week one and two. Test on staging first.
  • Check your critical plugins declare 7.0 compatibility before scheduling the upgrade — payment, forms, SEO and caching plugins especially.
  • Plan the window. A low-traffic slot in June, with a rollback path, beats a Friday-afternoon "update all" every time.
What we'd do about it

Upgrade — deliberately. 7.0's AI infrastructure will matter for how plugins and agents interact with your site over the next year, and falling multiple majors behind is its own risk. But sequence it: staging test, plugin audit, then production. If you're searching "web developers near me" to handle a stuck upgrade, ask candidates one question first: what's your rollback plan?

6. Chrome 149 beta: CSS gap decorations and friends

On the platform side, Chrome 149 entered beta with a genuinely useful CSS batch: gap decorations (styling the whitespace between flex and grid items — rules and dividers without pseudo-element hacks), shape() and path() support in shape-outside for text that wraps around real shapes, and WebSocket connections no longer blocking the back/forward cache — a quiet win for perceived speed on web apps. The web.dev May platform roundup collects the cross-browser status of each.

None of this is urgent, but gap decorations in particular will retire a whole class of divider hacks from design systems. Worth a note in your component backlog for when it reaches stable.

What this week means for your website strategy

Pull the threads together and the week points one direction for anyone scoping design or development work:

  • Quality is being re-priced, again. The core update is rewarding depth and first-hand experience; Google's AI guide says the same content wins citations. One investment serves both — and it starts at build time, in structure and speed, not in a post-launch "SEO phase".
  • Measurement is catching up to reality. With GA4 now splitting out AI Assistant traffic, "how much revenue does AI send us" stops being a guess. Instrument it before the channel gets big, not after.
  • Commerce is going agentic. Ads in AI Mode and cross-surface carts mean product data hygiene is now a growth lever. If your store's feeds, schema and speed haven't been audited since launch, that's the gap — and it's the sharpest brief you can hand Shopify development partners or any website development company you're evaluating right now.

Frequently asked questions

Should I change my website while the May 2026 core update is still rolling out?

No. The update that started 21 May is still processing, and mid-rollout positions — including the 29–30 May swings — can reverse before completion. Log what moved, rule out technical faults, and hold structural changes until Google confirms the rollout is done and you have a week of stable data.

Do I need GEO or AEO services now that Google published its AI search guide?

No. Google's own guide says there's no separate discipline for AI Overviews and AI Mode — the fundamentals are the same: crawlable HTML, clear structure, unique first-hand content, solid technical SEO. Anyone selling GEO as a separate line item is repackaging SEO done well.

Should I upgrade to WordPress 7.0 straight away?

Not on a revenue-critical site in week one. Raised PHP requirements and the admin rebuild mean plugin conflicts surface early. Test on staging, confirm your key plugins support 7.0, and schedule the upgrade in a low-traffic window with a rollback path.

How do I track AI chatbot traffic in Google Analytics now?

GA4 is rolling out a native AI Assistant channel that automatically groups visits from ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude and others under an ai-assistant medium. Check whether your property has it, annotate the date, and keep a manual segment for sources not yet covered, like Perplexity.

Will ads in AI Mode reduce my organic visibility?

Some queries will show sponsored conversational answers, adding competition for attention inside the answer surface. But organic citations still appear — and they go to quotable, well-structured, fast pages. It's a reason to invest in page quality and conversion, not to abandon organic.

The takeaway

A core update mid-rollout, a billion-user AI surface opening to ads, and Google itself writing down what wins in AI search: the week's message is that there's exactly one strategy left. Build a fast, structured, genuinely expert website; measure every channel honestly; and make every page earn its keep in revenue. Everything Google shipped this week rewards the sites that were already doing that — and re-prices the ones that weren't.
